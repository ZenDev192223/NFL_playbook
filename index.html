Running Plays

Control the line of scrimmage and set up everything else. A dominant run game dictates tempo, controls the clock, wears down defenders, and makes every play-action attempt dangerous. The effectiveness of every run play depends on your personnel — the right RB for the right scheme, and an offensive line that can execute.

RUN

Inside Zone

The offensive line blocks as a unit toward one side, each lineman taking a zone rather than a specific man. The running back reads the movement of the defensive line and cuts to whichever gap opens. The most widely used run play in professional football because it works from multiple formations and forces the defense to maintain gap discipline across the entire front.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Singleback, I-Formation, Pistol
Use When
1st & 10, reliable 4–6 yd gain needed; defense in 4-3 or Nickel
Beats: Cover 2, 4-3 Under, 3-4 Even
Weak vs: 46 Bear, dominant NT, slanting DL
Player Complexity
Mobile / Elusive RB: Thrives here — can bounce cutbacks without losing speed. Christian McCaffrey types excel.
Power RB (poor elusiveness): Must hit the first crease hard; has no patience for reading blocks. Force the hole or lose yards.
If your OL is weak: Collapse inside zone immediately — stunt-heavy DTs will blow this up before the RB can read anything.
Against elite interior DTs: Even a great RB cannot overcome a dominant defensive tackle who wins his one-on-one every snap.
Coach's Adaptation

If your RB is a power runner in an elusive-RB scheme, cut your run volume and rely more on misdirection. If your RB is a shifty back behind a dominant OL, inside zone should be your base call on first down.

RUN

Outside Zone (Stretch)

The entire offensive line steps laterally toward the play side, forcing defensive linemen to chase them sideways. The RB reads the perimeter and makes one decisive cut: bounce outside if the edge holds, or cut back against the grain when the defensive end overruns. The cutback is often the bigger gain.

Best Formations
Singleback, I-Formation, Shotgun
Use When
1st & 10; defense over-pursues or has slow LBs
Beats: Aggressive 4-3 Over, slow-LB 3-4
Weak vs: Speed edge rushers, contain-first schemes
Player Complexity
Fast, patient RB: The ideal carrier — can press the edge, wait for the cut, then explode. Speed without patience kills this play.
Slow or straight-line RB: This play is nearly unrunnable. A RB who cannot threaten the perimeter cannot set up the cutback lane.
Tall, slow OTs: If your OTs cannot reach the edge defenders laterally, the play collapses before the RB gets outside.
Against elite edge speed (EDGE rushers): A Von Miller-type can chase from the backside and make the tackle for loss even on a cutback.
RUN

Power (Power O)

A pulling guard wraps around to kick out the playside defensive end while a fullback leads through the hole to block the playside linebacker. Creates a 2-on-1 advantage at the point of attack. The most physical run play in football — the go-to call in short yardage and goalline situations.

Best Formations
I-Formation, Singleback, Goalline, Pistol
Use When
2nd & short, 3rd & 1, goalline, need physicality
Beats: 4-3 Under, 3-4 without strong interior
Weak vs: 46 Bear, slant into pulling guard's gap
Player Complexity
No fullback on roster: This play loses 40% of its effectiveness without the lead blocker. An H-back is an acceptable substitute but not ideal.
Power RB (Derrick Henry archetype): Perfect fit — one cut behind the pulling guard and the power back punishes arm tackles downfield.
Weak or slow pulling guard: If the guard cannot get around the formation and execute the kick-out in time, the play dies at the line.
Against elite edge speed that chases pullers: A fast EDGE defender can beat the pulling guard around the corner and blow up the play in the backfield.
RUN

Counter

The RB takes a step away from the play direction to freeze linebackers and pull them the wrong way. Two blockers — typically a guard and tackle — pull behind the line and lead through the backside gap. A pure misdirection play that punishes defenses that over-pursue.

Best Formations
Singleback, I-Formation, Pistol, Shotgun
Use When
After establishing zone runs; defense is chasing aggressively
Beats: Fast-flowing defenses, Cover 1 Man with crashing LBs
Weak vs: Patient gap-control defenses, 46 Bear
Player Complexity
Agile, change-of-direction RB: The setup step and late cut are everything. An RB with elite agility makes this look like a different play each time.
Straight-line speed RB: Struggles to sell the counter step convincingly. The freeze on linebackers is reduced, limiting the play's effectiveness.
Disciplined LBs who hold gaps: Disciplined linebackers do not bite on the initial misdirection — the counter lane closes before it opens.
RUN

Draw

The QB drops back as if passing, inviting pass rushers to commit upfield. The OL lets them flow past, then the QB hands off to the RB who runs through vacated interior gaps. Specifically designed to punish aggressive pass rushers who fly upfield on passing downs.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Singleback, Spread
Use When
2nd & long, 3rd & medium; after two consecutive passing plays
Beats: 4-2-5 Nickel, Dime, aggressive pass rush defenses
Weak vs: Base 4-3, 3-4 teams with gap integrity
Player Complexity
RB who can receive from shotgun: Critical — the RB must catch the handoff cleanly from depth in shotgun sets or fumbles occur under pressure.
QB sell of the fake: A QB who looks confused or panics in his drop-back actually sells this play worse — defenders read body language. A calm, deliberate drop is the deception.
If defense sends a spy LB: The draw's interior lanes close immediately. Audible to a quick pass or a run outside before the snap.
RUN

Sweep / Jet Sweep

Traditional sweep: RB receives a toss and attacks the perimeter with lead blockers. Jet sweep: a WR takes a handoff in full pre-snap motion, attacking the edge at top speed before the defense can react. Both plays live and die on the quality of edge blocking and perimeter speed.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, Wildcat
Use When
You have a fast WR/RB; defense is soft on the edge; 3 times/game max
Beats: Over-pursuing defenses, teams without a force player
Weak vs: Fast OLBs who set the edge, contain-first schemes
Player Complexity
4.3-speed WR on jet sweep: The entire point is to outrun the defense. A receiver with elite straight-line speed can outflank contain players before they react.
Slot WR who is a good blocker: Jet sweep works as an RPO threat — even if you don't hand off, the WR in motion demands the defense's attention.
Slow or undersized OTs: Without credible edge blocking, the contain player simply ignores the puller and makes the tackle at the line.
RUN

QB Sneak

The QB takes the snap and immediately drives forward behind the center and guards with no handoff. Statistically the highest-conversion short-yardage play in football — NFL teams convert over 80% of QB sneaks when fewer than 2 yards are needed. It is about leverage, not athleticism.

Best Formations
Under-center only — I-Formation, Goalline, Singleback
Use When
4th & inches, 3rd & 1, goalline — must-convert situations
Beats: Any defense — leverage wins, not scheme
Weak vs: Two massive DTs stacked on the center
Player Complexity
Big, strong QB (Lamar/Josh Allen physique): The sneak gains an extra 0.5–1 yards even against a stacked front because of physical power through contact.
Small or mobility-first QB: Still effective — the leverage advantage is structural, not personal. Even a shorter QB can generate yards through the line behind good center-guard synchronization.
Weak center: If your center cannot generate initial push, a powerful NT wins the leverage battle immediately. The sneak becomes a 0-yard play.
Cannot be run from shotgun: The QB must be under center. Any team trying this from the gun loses the leverage advantage entirely.
RUN

Trap

A DL is deliberately left unblocked on the initial snap, then a pulling guard comes from the opposite side and blindside-blocks him. The RB hits the trap hole created by this block. The unblocked DL thinks he has a free run at the QB — then gets trapped instead.

Best Formations
I-Formation, Singleback
Use When
Against aggressive DTs who fire upfield; after a passing down
Beats: Penetrating 4-3, aggressive interior pass rushers
Weak vs: Patient DL who controls gaps; 3-4 fronts
Player Complexity
Compact, decisive RB: The trap hole is narrow and brief. A north-south runner who hits the crease immediately gains 5–8 yards. A dancing RB kills the play.
Quick-twitch pulling guard: The pulling guard's speed to the trap point is everything. A slow guard means the DL recovers from the block and closes the hole.
Passing Plays

Modern NFL football is won and lost in the passing game. Understanding which routes attack which coverage shells, how your QB's arm strength and accuracy affect play selection, and how your WR's strengths dictate your route tree — that is the difference between a three-and-out and a touchdown drive.

PASS

Slant

The WR runs 3–5 steps upfield and cuts sharply inside at a 45-degree angle. The QB throws immediately after the cut. One of the quickest throws in football, designed to attack the middle of the field and beat man coverage with a break the cornerback cannot anticipate.

Best Formations
Any — Shotgun, Singleback, Spread
Use When
Against press man; 3rd & short; against blitzes
Beats: Cover 1 Man, press coverage, all-out blitzes
Weak vs: Cover 3 (MLB sits in window), Cover 2 man
Player Complexity
Quick-release QB: The slant is most deadly from a QB who can fire it on time. A QB with a slow release gives the LB time to close on the route after the cut.
Strong-armed QB with poor accuracy short: Avoid. Overthrowing a slant results in an interception against a zone dropping LB. Better to find a different underneath route.
Small, slot WR: Ideal carrier — quick-twitch route running and the ability to absorb contact crossing through traffic makes this lethal.
Large, physical WR: Can still run the slant effectively but must be targeted early. His size makes catching-in-traffic easier but his break speed is slower.
PASS

Hitch / Curl

The receiver sprints 5–7 yards upfield at full speed, stops abruptly, and turns back to face the QB. He becomes a stationary target in front of the coverage — the safest passing play in football and the most common 3rd-down chain-mover against soft zone.

Best Formations
Any — Shotgun, Spread, Singleback
Use When
3rd & 4–6; defense gives cushion pre-snap; 2-minute drill
Beats: Cover 2 zone, Cover 3 with bailing corners
Weak vs: Press man, pattern-match Cover 4
Player Complexity
QB with poor footwork or slow processing: The hitch must be thrown as the WR stops — late throws allow the CB who backed off to close and make a play on the ball.
WR with elite route-running precision: The hitch only works if the stop is sharp. A WR who rounds his routes gives corners an extra step to recover.
Against press coverage: Do not throw the hitch. The corner's jam disrupts the timing, and he can undercut the stop route for an interception.
PASS

Go Route / Fade

The WR sprints straight downfield at top speed. In the red zone this becomes a fade — the QB lofts to the back corner where only the WR can catch it. Outside the red zone a go route vertically stretches the defense and opens underneath routes by forcing safeties to respect deep threats.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, I-Formation (goalline fade)
Use When
Speed mismatch vs. a corner; red zone; clear underneath coverage
Beats: Cover 1 with speed mismatch, Cover 3 with soft corners
Weak vs: Cover 2 (safeties take deep halves), Quarters
Player Complexity
Weak-armed QB: Do not throw the deep go route — the ball hangs in the air and gives safeties time to close. Stick to the fade in the red zone where the throw is shorter.
Tall, jump-ball WR (6'3"+): The red zone fade becomes a 50-60% play rather than a coin flip — size and catch radius compensate for imperfect ball placement.
Speed-only WR with poor hands: The deep go route only works if the WR can catch the ball at top speed. Drops downfield are demoralizing and momentum-killers.
Against Cover 2: Never throw the go. The safety has the deep half and is waiting for it. Check the safeties every single time before pulling the trigger.
PASS

Crossing Route (Dig)

The WR runs 10–15 yards upfield, then breaks sharply across the middle parallel to the line of scrimmage. One of the most effective ways to attack zone coverage horizontally, and creates natural rubs against man coverage when paired with crossing routes from the opposite side.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, Singleback
Use When
Against zone; attacking the middle; pair with flat route
Beats: Cover 2, Cover 3 (soft zone), between safeties
Weak vs: Tampa 2 (MLB drops into lane), physical defenses
Player Complexity
WR who can take a hit: This route runs through a high-traffic area. A WR who alligator-arms crossing routes will drop balls and won't run this route with conviction next time.
QB who locks onto one receiver: Dangerous on crossing routes — the strong safety can read the QB's eyes and jump the route. Must look off the safety before throwing.
Physical CB who jams at the line: If the WR cannot get a clean release, the entire timing of the crossing route is destroyed.
PASS

Comeback Route

The WR runs deep (12–16 yards), then breaks back toward the sideline and the QB. The WR moves toward the QB while the CB who was running with him cannot stop fast enough to undercut the throw. Requires arm strength and WR precision.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Singleback, 2x2 formations
Use When
CB bails deep; against Cover 3 outside corners
Beats: Cover 3, Cover 4 with soft corners
Weak vs: Press man, aggressive corners who press at 15 yards
Player Complexity
Weak-armed QB: This throw has a tight window — the WR is moving back while the CB recovers. A QB without arm strength lets the CB close before the ball arrives.
WR with elite route precision: The break must be sudden and exact. A rounded comeback gives the corner one extra step and turns a completion into a contested ball.
If your WR is smaller/shorter: Against a physical press corner, the WR may not even get to the 14-yard depth before being disrupted. Check the coverage pre-snap.
PASS

Post Route

After 10–15 yards upfield, the WR breaks inside at 45 degrees toward the goalpost. Attacks the middle of the field and exploits single-high safety coverage — when one safety defends the middle alone, a post route challenges him to defend both sides simultaneously.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, Singleback
Use When
One deep safety (Cover 1/3); speed mismatch; red zone from the 20
Beats: Cover 1, Cover 3 (safety occupied)
Weak vs: Cover 2, Tampa 2, Quarters (safety help inside)
Player Complexity
Speed WR vs. single coverage: The post is a weapon. A 4.3-speed receiver running a sharp post against one safety is a near-automatic first down or touchdown.
Slow-developing QB read: Against Cover 1, the post must be thrown on time — not early, not late. A QB who hesitates allows the single safety to recover to the hash.
Against Cover 2: Never throw the post — two safeties own the inside deep. Pre-snap, count safeties. Two safeties = throw corner, not post.
PASS

Corner Route

The WR runs 10–12 yards upfield, then breaks toward the back corner of the end zone or sideline at 45 degrees. The direct counter to the post — designed specifically to attack Cover 2 by hitting the deep corner between the corner and safety where neither can cover in time.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, Singleback
Use When
Against Cover 2; red zone from 10–20 yards; safety rolls to post
Beats: Cover 2, Cover 1 with middle safety
Weak vs: Cover 3, Cover 4 Quarters
Player Complexity
Post-corner combo: Running a post on the inside WR simultaneously draws the safety inside — the outside WR then runs the corner into an open void. This is one of the most reliably open 2-route combos vs. Cover 2.
QB must throw to the front shoulder: The WR is running away from the QB and toward the boundary — an inside throw is an interception. Placement discipline matters enormously on this route.
PASS

Seam Route

A TE, RB, or slot WR runs straight upfield along the hash mark — the seam of the defense. Exploits the gap between linebackers who lack deep speed and safeties playing wide. It is a persistent structural weakness in most zone coverages that every offense should attack weekly.

Best Formations
Shotgun with TE, 2-TE sets, Singleback
Use When
Against Cover 2 (safeties play wide); TE faster than LBs
Beats: Cover 2, Cover 4 Quarters (TE runs free)
Weak vs: Tampa 2 (MLB drops into seam), Cover 1 with seam safety
Player Complexity
Athletic TE (Travis Kelce archetype): The seam route is arguably the most dangerous play in football with a receiving TE who can threaten the middle of the field. Linebackers cannot cover him, safeties must cheat — which opens everything else.
Blocking-first TE: Running the seam with a TE who cannot separate from a linebacker wastes a route. Use this TE in 2-TE blocking sets instead and find your seam threat in the slot WR.
QB with touch on mid-level throws: The seam throw requires touch — not velocity. A QB who rifle-passes everything will often throw the seam into the receiver's body instead of over his shoulder.
PASS

Wheel Route

An RB or slot WR runs a flat route toward the sideline, then turns upfield into a go route along the boundary. Linebackers who drop to cover the flat are left chasing the RB deep down the sideline — a matchup they almost never win. The premier linebacker-killer in football.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Singleback — any with a receiving RB
Use When
Fast RB vs. LB; Cover 2 (corner takes flat, safety takes post)
Beats: Cover 2, Cover 3, zone with a LB in the flat
Weak vs: Man coverage with safety on RB, pattern-match Cover 4
Player Complexity
Receiving RB (Christian McCaffrey type): This is a weapon. A RB who can threaten the flat and the deep sideline simultaneously forces the defense to allocate a safety to the RB — opening other routes.
Power RB with limited receiving ability: If your RB cannot reliably catch out of the backfield, the wheel route becomes a decoy only. Pair it with the TE seam instead and ignore the wheel concept.
If the LB sits in the flat: Throw the flat route immediately for 4–5 yards. The wheel concept creates a choice — the LB takes the flat, throw deep. LB goes deep, throw flat.
PASS

Screen Pass (RB Screen)

The QB takes a short drop, then dumps a pass behind the line of scrimmage to a RB who has let pass rushers run past him. Offensive linemen release and sprint to become blockers in space. Specifically designed to neutralize aggressive pass rushes and turn blitzes into big gains.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Singleback — quality RB essential
Use When
Heavy blitz packages; after defense has gotten to QB consistently
Beats: Zero blitz, Cover 0, all-out blitz packages
Weak vs: Spy LB; reading LBs in Cover 3
Player Complexity
Athletic, fast OL: The screen only works if your guards and tackles can get outside and create running lanes in space. A slow OL means the RB catches the ball with no blockers ahead of him.
RB who can make one man miss: The screen delivers the ball in space, but an RB with no elusiveness will be tackled immediately by the spy linebacker or a fast closing DB.
QB's sell: A QB who looks panicked or who dump-offs the screen immediately reveals the play. The QB must look downfield convincingly for 1–1.5 seconds before releasing to the RB.
PASS

Play-Action Pass

The QB fakes a handoff before throwing. Linebackers and safeties conditioned to stop the run freeze for a moment, creating coverage windows that do not exist on a standard dropback. Play-action is only as effective as the run game that precedes it.

Best Formations
I-Formation, Singleback, Pistol (under-center preferred)
Use When
After establishing run game; against LBs who flow on run action
Beats: Cover 2, Cover 3, any zone with run-respecting LBs
Weak vs: Dime/Prevent alignments; defenses ignoring the run
Player Complexity
Mobile QB: Play-action becomes a run-pass option — the QB can keep if the defense crashes too hard on the fake. Defenses must account for this and often cannot sell out on the fake.
Pure pocket-passer QB: The play-action fake only works if the OL sells the run block. A pocket QB who has not established the run game gets no freeze from linebackers — they read pass immediately.
Must have established the run: Play-action without a run game is theater. If you have not run the ball successfully in this game, linebackers will not bite on any fake — they simply drop into coverage.
PASS

Four Verticals

All four eligible receivers sprint straight downfield simultaneously. The QB reads across the field, finding whichever receiver is open in a zone seam or wins a one-on-one matchup. The purest deep passing concept and the best answer to a defense overloading one side of the field.

Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, 4-wide
Use When
Against Cover 2; 3rd & long; defense playing all-short zone
Beats: Cover 1 Man, Cover 2 (seam), Cover 3 (post behind corner)
Weak vs: Cover 4 Quarters, Dime with four DBs deep
Player Complexity
Elite speed at all four spots: With genuine speed across the board, the defense cannot bracket any one receiver without leaving another open — the concept breaks down every coverage shell it faces.
Strong-armed QB: Four verticals requires the QB to throw into tight windows downfield against fast-closing safeties. A weak arm means underthrown balls and interceptions.
QB with accurate deep ball: If your QB's deep accuracy is poor, run the four-verticals concept to draw coverage and attack the shorter crossing routes that open underneath — not the deep ball itself.
PASS

Quick Out / Flat Route

The WR runs a short horizontal route toward the sideline, 3–5 yards downfield. One of the fastest throws in football — a 1-step drop and release. Used to move chains reliably, get playmakers in space, and as the primary hot route against interior blitzes.

Best Formations
Any — Shotgun, Singleback, Spread
Use When
Press man (out beats the jam); vs. blitz; 2-minute drill clock stop
Beats: Cover 1 Man, inside blitzes, cushion-giving defenses
Weak vs: CBs with outside leverage, pattern-match flat coverage
Player Complexity
Quick-twitch slot WR or RB: The flat route delivers the ball in space at the line of scrimmage — a receiver who can make one cut and gain 6 yards after the catch turns this into a dangerous weapon.
QB who can throw while moving: Against an incoming blitz, the QB may not have time to set his feet. An accurate throw on the move to the flat is a required skill for this route to work under pressure.
Trick & Special Plays

Trick plays are precision weapons — not gimmicks. Used sparingly at exactly the right moment they break a defense's will and swing momentum. Used randomly, they are turnovers waiting to happen. Every trick play works better after you have established something the defense believes in.

TRICK

Flea Flicker

QB hands off to the RB who takes a few steps as if it is a run. The RB then pitches back to the QB, who has set up in the pocket. Safeties and LBs crash on the run fake, leaving WRs completely open deep.

Use When
After establishing the run; safety cheating up hard on run support
Avoid When
Before you have run the ball; defense hasn't bitten on run fakes all game
Player Complexity
RB who can pitch cleanly: The pitch-back must be precise. A fumbled or off-target pitch in the backfield is a catastrophic turnover at the worst possible moment.
Strong-armed QB: After the pitch-back, the QB must immediately throw deep — the pocket collapses fast once the OL realizes it is a pass. A strong arm means not waiting for receivers to fully separate.
Fast deep WR: Target your fastest WR on a deep post or go route — he has at minimum a 2-step head start on any defender. Do not waste this play on a possession receiver.
TRICK

End-Around / Reverse

A WR in motion receives a handoff and attacks the opposite edge from the initial run flow. The defense chases the original run direction and leaves the backside edge unblocked.

Use When
After aggressive pursuit toward the run side; when WR is faster than edge defenders
Avoid When
Against backside contain; wet conditions; once per game maximum
Player Complexity
4.3-speed WR: This is the only meaningful attribute on the end-around. If your WR cannot outrun the defense's contain player to the edge, this play loses 5 yards every time.
WR who can also throw: The reverse pass off this play creates an additional layer of deception — even a single incomplete pass attempt out of this look makes the defense second-guess pursuing aggressively.
TRICK

Double Pass (WR Pass)

The QB throws a lateral to a WR who then throws downfield to a second receiver. The entire defense vacates deep coverage assuming it is a sweep or run play.

Use When
Near goalline; after reverse has worked; 4th down trick situation
Avoid When
WR has poor throwing ability; wet conditions; defense not biting on run fakes
Player Complexity
WR with QB-level throw accuracy: This play only works if the WR can reliably throw a catchable ball. A WR with poor arm mechanics will either overthrow, underthrow, or flutter-ball the throw right into a defender.
The receiving WR must be open downfield: If the defense has a deep safety who does not bite on the lateral, the second WR will be covered. The play is a turnover waiting to happen in that scenario.
TRICK

Statue of Liberty

The QB raises his arm as if throwing, freezing all defenders in pass coverage, then hands off or pitches behind his back to a RB or WR sweeping in the other direction. One of the oldest plays in football — pure leverage on pass-anticipating defenders.

Use When
2-point conversions; goalline; 4th & short; after play-action works repeatedly
Avoid When
Alert zone defenses; trailing by more than a TD late
Player Complexity
Against man coverage: Man defenders follow their receivers into fake pass coverage — they do not read the lateral. This is the ideal scenario for the Statue of Liberty to gain 5+ yards.
Against zone: Zone defenders hold their keys and read the ball — they will see the lateral and close fast. The play often loses yards against well-coached zone teams.
TRICK

QB Draw (Designed QB Run)

The QB takes a full pass-drop, surveys as if reading routes, then tucks and runs through the middle of the defense. Blockers set as if pass blocking, then pivot to create a lane. Exploits LBs who drop into zone and pass rushers who fly upfield.

Use When
Against Nickel/Dime with light box; LBs drop deep every snap; mobile QB
Avoid When
Pocket-passer QB; defense has a dedicated QB spy
Player Complexity
Mobile QB (85+ speed): This is a genuine weapon with a QB who can threaten the edge. Lamar Jackson, Josh Allen, and Jalen Hurts types turn this into an unpredictable run-pass threat every single snap.
Pocket-passer QB: Do not call this play. A QB with 65 speed running into an unblocked linebacker is a fumble or a 10-yard loss. Replace this play with a draw to the RB instead.
Against a spy LB: The moment a defense assigns a LB to spy the QB, the QB draw becomes obsolete. Audible to a quick pass that attacks the vacated spy spot.
Defensive Formations

Defense is about taking away what the offense does best, then making them do something they are uncomfortable with. The right defensive formation gives you a structural advantage before the ball is even snapped. Each formation requires specific personnel — deploying the wrong defenders in the wrong scheme neutralizes even elite talent.

DEF

4-3 Defense

Four DL (two DTs, two DEs) across the front with three LBs behind. The most common base defensive alignment in the NFL. Four-man front provides consistent pass rush without extra blitzers; three LBs give flexibility against both run and short passing routes.

Use When
1st & 10; balanced offensive opponents; early downs needing to stop both run and pass
Avoid When
Spread offenses with 4+ WRs; dynamic passing attacks needing more DBs
Beats: Power run offenses, 2-TE sets, predictable tendencies
Weak vs: Spread 4-5 WR sets (LBs covering WRs), no-huddle pace
Personnel Complexity
Two elite DTs: They are the most important defenders in this front. If your DTs cannot win one-on-one against guards and centers, the entire run defense collapses.
Mike LB: The signal-caller of the entire defense. A Mike who cannot read and diagnose quickly creates hesitation across the entire unit. He must be your smartest defensive player.
Weak coverage LBs: Against a spread offense, your LBs will be asked to cover slot WRs in man coverage. If they cannot, sub to Nickel immediately — do not stay in 4-3 and give up easy completions.
DEF

3-4 Defense

Three DL (NT and two ends) with four LBs behind. The extra LB creates ambiguity in pass rush: any one of the four can blitz and the offense cannot know which one will go. Requires a dominant NT who can control two blockers alone.

Use When
Elite OLBs who can rush and cover; disguising blitz packages
Avoid When
Without a dominant NT; against elite interior offensive lines
Beats: Offenses that cannot handle disguised blitzes, predictable run games
Weak vs: Power offenses that isolate NT, OLs that ID the blitzing LB pre-snap
Personnel Complexity
The Nose Tackle is non-negotiable: Without a true NT who can two-gap both guards simultaneously, the 3-4 fails entirely. A 4-3 DT plugged into the NT spot will be dominated and the run defense collapses.
OLBs must be hybrid pass-rusher/coverage players: The 3-4 OLB is the most complex position in football. He must stand up, rush, drop, and cover — all in the same game. Specialists who only rush give away your call pre-snap.
DEF

Nickel Defense (4-2-5)

One LB is replaced by a fifth defensive back — the "nickel" cornerback — creating a 4-2-5 look. The most commonly used formation in the modern NFL, used on over 60% of all defensive snaps leaguewide because most offenses use three or more WRs on a majority of plays.

Use When
3rd & medium; offense uses 3+ WRs; most obvious passing situations
Avoid When
2-TE heavy sets; goalline; clear run-heavy downs
Beats: 3-WR spread offenses, pass-heavy attacks on 3rd down
Weak vs: Power run with 2 TEs, goalline, RB after showing 3-WR
Personnel Complexity
Nickel CB quality: Your fifth DB must be able to cover the opponent's slot WR — often one of the most difficult coverage matchups in football. A slow or weak-coverage nickel gets exposed every third-down snap.
Two remaining LBs: The two LBs left in the Nickel must cover the run gap aggressively since they have no help from a third LB. If your LBs are slow or undisciplined, the nickel bleeds run yards.
DEF

Dime Defense (4-1-6)

A sixth DB replaces another LB, creating a 4-1-6 look. A pure pass-coverage defense used almost exclusively on obvious passing downs. Sacrifices run-stopping ability entirely in exchange for blanket coverage across all passing routes.

Use When
3rd & long (8+ yards); 4-WR sets; obvious 2-minute passing situations
Avoid When
Any run-likely situation; goalline; 1st and 2nd down vs. balanced offenses
Beats: 4-wide spread attacks, 3rd & long, no-huddle 2-minute
Weak vs: Any run play — one LB means the run game devastates this front
Personnel Complexity
If opponent runs into Dime: You will give up 8–12 yards with a single safety in the box and one LB trying to tackle a back with momentum. Sub out immediately the moment you see run tendency on 3rd & 8+.
The lone LB: He must function as both a pass rusher and the only run-stopper. An athletic, instinctive LB in this role is critical — a one-dimensional LB is a liability in Dime.
DEF

Prevent Defense

Seven or more DBs align deep across the field with only three or four rushers. Every defender drops 10–15 yards off the line. The goal: prevent any single explosive play from ending the game. Short completions are conceded willingly — no touchdowns is the only priority.

Use When
Leading by 8+ points with under 2 minutes left; protecting a 2-score lead at end of half
Avoid When
Any time earlier in the game — it surrenders chains and kills momentum
Beats: Deep shots, single-play TD attempts, Hail Mary
Weak vs: Short dink-and-dunk — gives those up freely
Critical Coaching Note
Never use prevent with 2+ minutes left: The math does not work. A team needs ~6 plays to score from midfield. 2 minutes gives them 8–10 plays. Prevent gives away the first 5 plays — you hand them the game.
Always rush at least 3 players: A completely unblocked QB will pick apart a prevent defense with no pass rush by simply setting and throwing every play. At minimum, three rushers to force some movement.
DEF

46 Defense (Bear)

Eight defenders near the line of scrimmage with only three in coverage. Developed by Buddy Ryan with the 1985 Chicago Bears — the most dominant defensive team in NFL history. Puts maximum pressure on the OL by overloading every gap simultaneously. Extremely aggressive, extremely risky.

Use When
Short yardage goalline stops; 3rd & short must-stop; against young or inexperienced QBs
Avoid When
Against an explosive passing attack; quick-release QB with hot routes; obvious passing downs
Beats: Power run offenses, 2-TE sets, inexperienced QBs
Weak vs: Quick passing game (screens, slants, hot routes), mobile QBs
Personnel Complexity
The three coverage defenders: With only three defenders in coverage, your three DBs must be elite man-coverage players. One blown assignment against a veteran QB equals a touchdown. Do not run this with weak coverage corners.
Mobile QB burns this automatically: A scrambling QB finds the massive open space behind eight box defenders immediately. If the opponent has a running QB, never call the 46. Sub to a zone that maintains contain first.

Game Situation Guide

Knowing the plays is only half the battle. Knowing which play to call in which situation — and adapting that call to your roster's actual strengths and weaknesses — is what separates average play-callers from great ones.

01 1st & 10 — Standard Down
Key Principle

Balance is the weapon. If the defense knows whether you are running or passing on 1st down, they own every drive. Vary your personnel and formations to prevent tendencies.

Best Play Calls
Inside Zone, Outside Zone, Play-Action, Slant, Hitch — stay balanced
Best Formations
Singleback, I-Formation, Shotgun — vary each series
Adapt to Your Roster
If your RB is elite: Run on 1st down more often (55-45 run-pass split) to establish physical dominance and make every play-action devastating.
If your QB is elite but RB is average: Favor 1st-down passes to put the game on your best player's arm. Use short, high-percentage routes — slant, hitch, quick out — to gain 5–7 yards and face 2nd & manageable.
If your WRs are poor: Run the ball on 1st down regardless of the defensive front. Asking a weak WR group to beat press coverage on 1st down is a guaranteed three-and-out.
02 3rd & Short (1–3 yards)
Key Principle

The QB sneak converts over 80% of the time on 3rd & 1. Use it. Trust it. Do not get creative when you just need a yard — creativity is for when plays are open, not when you need 12 inches.

Best Play Calls
QB Sneak, Power run, Slant, Quick Out
Best Formations
I-Formation, Goalline, Jumbo — extra blockers
Adapt to Your Roster
Strong, physical QB: The sneak. Every time. There is no better 3rd & 1 call in football regardless of the defensive alignment.
Undersized or immobile QB: Power run behind the fullback becomes your primary option. The physical risk to a small QB on a sneak against a stacked front must be weighed.
No fullback on the roster: The quick slant to your best WR becomes your primary conversion tool on 3rd & short when you lack the personnel for power runs.
03 3rd & Long (7+ yards)
Key Principle

Expect the blitz. Have a hot route ready. The screen pass converts a blitz into a big gain — the defense is giving you a free play by bringing extra rushers on a long-yardage down.

Best Play Calls
Four Verticals, Crossing Routes, Comeback, Screen (if blitz)
Best Formations
Shotgun, Spread, 4-wide
Adapt to Your Roster
Elite WR1 in single coverage: Four verticals or the go route. Your best WR against one corner is a win — throw it to him even if the window is tight. Elite WRs make contested catches.
Average WR group: Crossing routes and mesh concepts create natural rubs and open windows that do not require individual separation ability. Take the crossing route at 8–10 yards and fight for the first down after the catch.
Poor QB arm strength: Stay away from deep routes on 3rd & 10+. Throw crossing routes and comebacks at 10–12 yards where the throw distance does not require a rifle arm. Manage the situation instead of forcing the deep ball.
04 Red Zone (Inside the 20)
Key Principle

The field compresses in the red zone — routes cannot develop the same way. Attack the corners of the end zone and exploit the leverage the compressed field gives you in the run game.

Best Play Calls
Fade (corner of end zone), Slant, Back-shoulder fade, Power run, Seam to TE
Best Formations
I-Formation (inside the 5), Shotgun Trips (10–20 out), Goalline
Adapt to Your Roster
Tall, physical WR (6'3"+): The back-shoulder fade is your primary red zone weapon. This throw requires QB placement discipline — outside shoulder, back corner — and a WR who can high-point the ball above the CB.
Athletic TE who can win vs. LBs: The seam route to the TE in the red zone is a nearly unstoppable play against Cover 2. The TE outrunning the linebacker into the end zone is a daily occurrence at the NFL level.
No red zone receiver: Rely on the run game inside the 10. Power and QB sneak near the goalline are simpler and more reliable than a contested fade to a possession receiver who cannot win against press coverage.
05 Two-Minute Drill
Key Principle

Out routes stop the clock. Slants gain yards fast. The spike is a legal timeout. Manage the clock and the timeouts simultaneously — not just the ball.

Best Play Calls
Out routes (clock stop), Slant, Crossing route, Spike
Best Formations
Spread, Shotgun, no-huddle — maximum tempo
Adapt to Your Roster
Quick-release QB: The two-minute drill is where quick-release QBs like Patrick Mahomes dominate — no pressure, no hesitation, ball out in 1.5 seconds to the correct route. The clock is the only real defender.
Slow-processing QB: Simplify the route trees to 2-option reads maximum. The two-minute drill is not the time for complex reads. Quick out, slant, spike — three plays, run them on repeat until you score.
No timeouts remaining: Out routes become mandatory. Any play that does not result in either a first down or an out-of-bounds completion is a wasted down and a lost game.
06 4th & 1 (Must Convert)
Key Principle

The QB sneak is the highest-conversion play in football on 4th & 1. If your QB is under center, this is the call. There is no situation in which creativity outperforms a statistically 80%+ play.

Best Play Calls
QB Sneak, Power run, Quick slant
Best Formations
Goalline, Jumbo, under-center I-Formation
Adapt to Your Roster
The sneak requirement: Aligning under center is a prerequisite. Any team in shotgun on 4th & 1 should audible under center immediately. The sneak from the gun is not a sneak — it is a QB run that loses 3 yards.
If defense stacks two DTs on the center: The power run is your counter. Pull the guard and create a crease at the B-gap rather than fighting through the center-nose tackle matchup that the defense has won before the snap.
07 Protecting a Lead (Up 14+ Late)
Key Principle

Every second that ticks off the clock is a victory. Run the ball. Force them to use timeouts. The opponent needs scores, not yards — let them have 5-yard gains on defense, never let them score touchdowns.

Best Play Calls (Offense)
Inside Zone, Power run, Screen pass — clock killers
Best Formations (Offense)
I-Formation, Singleback — power sets that eat clock
Adapt to Your Roster
Physical RB: Give him 25+ carries. The clock is your offense's best friend. A power RB grinding out 3–4 yard gains eats 40+ seconds per series and forces the opponent into a desperation mode.
No reliable run game: Short, safe passes to the flat and checkdowns serve the same purpose. The goal is not yards — it is first downs that force the defense to burn their final timeouts.
Avoid deep passes entirely: Incomplete passes stop the clock — they are almost as bad as a turnover when protecting a lead. Throw only to open checkdowns and accept the short gain.
08 Facing a Heavy Blitz
Key Principle

A blitz gives you a numbers advantage somewhere. Hot routes flip the weapon back on the defense. Every blitz leaves a receiver or RB open — pre-snap adjustments find them before the ball is even snapped.

Best Play Calls
Screen pass, Slant (hot route), Quick flat, QB Draw
Best Formations
Spread, Empty backfield — force coverage on 5+ receivers
Adapt to Your Roster
Quick-release QB: Blitzes are not a problem — a QB who can identify and throw to the hot route in under 1.5 seconds turns every blitz into a 6-yard gain and an embarrassment for the defensive coordinator.
Slow-processing QB: Blitzes are devastating. Simplify: one pre-snap read (where is the blitz coming from?), one adjustment (hot route away from the blitz), one throw. Two-read progressions are impossible against a 5-man rush in the face.
Mobile QB vs. blitz: The QB Draw is lethal against a heavy blitz — 6 defenders rushing means only 5 defenders in coverage, leaving wide open lanes in the middle of the field. One cut and the QB is in the secondary.
Quarter-by-Quarter Guide

Every quarter of a football game demands a different strategic mindset. The plays that win in the 1st quarter are often the wrong calls in the 4th. Understanding how the game state evolves — and how to adapt your strategy at each phase — is what separates elite play-callers from reactive ones.

1st Quarter — Information & Establishment

The first quarter is not about winning. It is about gathering information, establishing physical presence, and concealing your full offensive and defensive arsenal for later use.

Opening Drive: Set Your Identity

The opening drive is a statement. If you want to be a physical run team, run the ball on 1st down three consecutive times. If you want to dictate a tempo game, spread the defense and attack quickly. Never open with trick plays or deep shots — you have not earned either yet and the risk-reward is catastrophically one-sided.

Watch how the defense lines up on your first 4–5 plays. Are they in Cover 2? Cover 3? How many men are in the box? This information shapes every play call for the next three quarters.

Testing the Defense's Coverage Shell

Use route concepts that reveal coverage: a hitch/flat combo tells you if it is zone (the flat is open) or man (the corner follows the hitch). A post route tells you if there is one safety or two. The first quarter is reconnaissance — you are building a map of the defense's tendencies that you will exploit in the 2nd and 4th quarters.

Establishing Run-Pass Balance

A 50-50 run-pass split on 1st downs in the 1st quarter makes every subsequent play-action attempt in the 3rd and 4th quarter dangerous. If you abandon the run early, you sacrifice all play-action credibility for the rest of the game. Run the ball even when it is not working perfectly — establishment is the goal, not yards per carry.

Defensive Strategy in Q1

Stay in base defense on early downs. Do not reveal your blitz packages in Q1 — save them for 3rd & long situations in Q2 and Q4 when the opponent is in a predictable passing situation. In Q1, your goal is to not give up big plays, not to create them. Force 3-and-outs with disciplined gap control and coverage responsibilities.

If You Score First

Do not abandon what worked on the opening scoring drive. Repetition builds pressure — a defense that has already been beaten by your inside zone once will begin to over-adjust, which opens the counter play. Early leads in Q1 should cause you to run more, not pass more.

If You Fall Behind Early

An early deficit in Q1 does not require panic. Seven points is one drive — you have 45+ minutes of football remaining. Do not abandon the run game after one bad series. Resist the urge to go deep on every play. The teams that fall into 3-score deficits in Q1 are almost always the ones who started throwing deep on every down after the first score against them.

2nd Quarter — Exploitation & Adjustment

The second quarter is where the information gathered in Q1 becomes weapons. Attack the coverage shells and individual matchups you identified early. The halftime clock creates urgency at the end of this quarter.

Attacking What Q1 Revealed

If Cover 2 was the primary coverage in Q1, your 2nd quarter play sheet should be loaded with corner routes, seam routes, and crossing routes that attack Cover 2 structurally. If the defense played Cover 3 with bailing corners, hitch and comeback routes are automatic chain-movers. Use your Q1 intelligence now or lose it forever at halftime.

Introducing Your First Wrinkles

Q2 is when your first non-standard plays should appear. If outside zone has worked repeatedly, the counter run belongs in your Q2 script. If you have thrown the hitch three times, the play-action deep ball off the same action is now ready. Trick plays should not appear until at least Q2 — and only if you have established the setup play first.

End-of-Half Two-Minute Drive

The two-minute drill at the end of the first half is one of the most important sequences in football. Have your two-minute script prepared before the game — when the clock hits 2:00, every play call should be pre-scripted rather than improvised. Use out routes to stop the clock, slants to gain yards quickly, and the spike only when you have time to run a play from a good position. The field goal is always on the table — do not gamble for the touchdown if a field goal ties or gives you a lead heading into halftime.

Halftime Adjustments Preview

As Q2 ends, mentally catalog three things: (1) what run plays gained positive yards, (2) which coverage shell the defense used most frequently, and (3) which of your receivers was most open on which routes. Your halftime adjustment is already decided — you are just confirming it in the final Q2 series.

Defensive Strategy in Q2

Introduce your first blitz packages in Q2 — specifically on 3rd & long situations where you know the opponent will pass. Do not show the blitz alignment pre-snap until Q2 or later — any blitz packages revealed in Q1 have already been accounted for by the opposing offensive coordinator at halftime. Save at least one blitz concept for Q4.

3rd Quarter — Halftime Adjustments Take Hold

The 3rd quarter is the most important 15 minutes of the game for the team that made better halftime adjustments. Both teams have seen each other's tendencies — the adjustment battle is the real contest now.

Opening the 3rd Quarter

The team that receives the second-half kickoff has an enormous advantage if they score on the opening drive. This erases halftime momentum and forces the opponent to immediately play from behind again. The opening Q3 drive should contain your highest-percentage, most-established plays from Q1 and Q2 — not new wrinkles. Execute what works before the defense has a chance to see your new adjustments.

Countering the Defense's Halftime Adjustment

The defense almost certainly changed something at halftime. If they were in Cover 2, expect Cover 3 or quarters to start Q3. If they blitzed rarely in Q1-Q2, expect it in Q3. Your first three plays of Q3 will reveal the new defensive scheme — read the coverage on these three plays before committing to your adjusted game plan. Do not assume the defense is still running what they showed in Q1.

The Play-Action Window

If you ran the ball effectively in Q1 and Q2, the Q3 play-action game is at maximum lethality. The first play-action shot of the game should come in Q3 — at least two full quarters after establishing the run. The linebacker who has seen inside zone 12 times will commit hard on the fake, creating deep windows that do not exist anywhere else in the game.

Trick Play Timing

Q3 is the ideal window for a well-set-up trick play. The flea flicker after establishing the run game, or the reverse after outside zone has worked repeatedly, hits the defense when they are most committed to their run-pursuit keys. Q3 trick plays are most effective early in the quarter — the defense is still locked into their Q2 pursuit habits before they fully process the new half.

Score Management in Q3

A two-score lead entering Q3 should shift your offensive philosophy toward clock control. A one-score game demands continued aggression. Teams that are tied or down by one score in Q3 must not become conservative — the 4th quarter advantage of having momentum and a lead is worth calculated aggression in the third.

Defensive Adjustments in Q3

If the offense has beaten your coverage shell all game, Q3 is when you must change it. Staying in the same losing coverage for 30 minutes of real football is a choice to lose the game. Rotate to a new coverage shell, introduce a new blitz look, and change your pre-snap alignment habits — create confusion before the offense adjusts to your new scheme.

4th Quarter — Decisive Execution Under Maximum Pressure

Every second of the 4th quarter has unique strategic meaning. The game situation — score margin, time remaining, timeouts — dictates every play call. Mental and physical discipline separates winners from losers here.

Opening Q4: Protect vs. Attack

Up by 10+ with 15 minutes left: Shift to clock control. Run the ball on first and second down in every series. Accept 3-and-outs as long as you consume 50+ seconds per series. Every drive that ends in a punt at the opponent's 20 has consumed 3–4 minutes of game time.

Down by any amount with 15 minutes left: There is no urgency yet — play your normal game. A team trailing by 10 with 15 minutes remaining has approximately 6 possessions left. Stay disciplined; do not abandon the run game to panic-throw on every down.

10 Minutes Remaining — Critical Threshold

At 10 minutes, the game state must actively change your play-calling. Trailing by 7–10: Continue mixing run and pass but incorporate more vertical routes that can score quickly. Trailing by 14–17: Increase pass volume significantly — you need two scores and time is becoming a genuine constraint. Leading by 14+: Full clock-control mode. The run game is your offense. Accept anything short of a touchdown on every defensive snap.

5 Minutes Remaining — Timeout Economy

At 5 minutes, timeouts are currency. If you are trailing, count your timeouts and the opponent's. Each timeout adds approximately 40 seconds of game time management flexibility. Save all three timeouts for the final two minutes — burning them at the 4-minute mark is burning a life preserver before the storm.

If you are leading, force the opponent to use their timeouts early. Run the ball in the middle of the field, accept clock-killing tackles, and make them burn their timeouts stopping the clock for you.

2 Minutes Remaining — Trailing

Two-minute drill execution. Every play must either gain a first down, stop the clock, or score. Do not run the ball — the clock runs after nearly every run play. Out routes stop the clock and keep the ball in bounds. The slant gains 6–8 yards fast. The spike after a big gain kills time and resets the play clock without burning a timeout.

If you have no timeouts: Out routes only — every play must go out of bounds or result in a first down. One in-bounds completion where the receiver is tackled can end the game. Coach your receivers to sprint out of bounds even on 5-yard gains.

2 Minutes Remaining — Leading

Clock management above all. Run the ball in the middle of the field — not toward the sideline where the offense can stop the clock. Take the sack if pressure comes — an intentional sack in the middle of the field runs more clock than a throwaway. Do not throw deep — incomplete passes stop the clock for the opponent. Every incompletion is a gift to the team trying to come back.

Final Drive — Down by 6 or Less

If trailing by 6 or fewer with one final possession, the entire offense has one purpose: reach the end zone. A field goal does not win the game — design your play calls around routes that score touchdowns, not routes that move the chains. Go routes, seam routes, back-shoulder fades, and the post route become your priority calls because they result in touchdowns when completed, not just first downs.

If you reach the red zone trailing by 6 with time running out and no timeouts: do not spike the ball. Every spike wastes one play from inside the 20. Run a play immediately — an incomplete pass stops the clock just as effectively as a spike while giving you a chance to score.

Hail Mary — Last Play

A Hail Mary requires specific execution. Your three best WRs line up on the same side — overwhelming one safety with three jump-ball threats. The QB throws high and into the back of the end zone. The offense's only job is to get bodies near the ball — tips and deflections off one WR can still be caught by another. Never throw the Hail Mary to one side and leave only one receiver in the end zone — numbers matter on a desperation play more than any route concept in football.

Defensive Protect-the-Lead in Q4

Leading by 8+ with under 4 minutes: prevent is appropriate only inside 2 minutes. Between 2 and 4 minutes, play normal defense — do not give up the short passes that let the offense march downfield without burning clock. Force them to use plays, not give them chains freely. Between 4 and 8 minutes with a 2-score lead: still blitz on 3rd & long — you want to force punts, not give up 3rd-down conversions by sitting in a soft zone.

Receiving TE (Move TE)
STRSeam route, wheel route from the backfield, crossing route, red zone favorite — the hardest matchup to defend in the NFL because no linebacker can cover him and no safety can handle his size.
WKRun blocking — a pure receiving TE who cannot block is a liability on run plays. Tip the defense to pass-first formations when he is the lone TE.
Blocking TE (Inline TE)
STRI-Formation power runs, play-action protection, goalline. His presence makes every run play on his side more effective by adding a functional blocker at the edge.
WKAny route beyond 8 yards — a blocking TE in a seam route is a decoy at best and a wasted route at worst. Target him only on short checkdowns when he leaks into the flat.

Offensive Line Profiles

Zone-Blocking OL
STRInside zone, outside zone, draw — any concept requiring lateral movement and combo blocking. Mobile linemen who reach defenders and climb to the second level.
WKPower and trap concepts require pulling guards and down blocking — zone linemen often lack the mass to win these one-on-one blocking situations in short yardage.
Gap-Blocking OL (Power)
STRPower, Counter, Trap, goalline — dominant at the point of attack when winning individual matchups. Creates instant lanes for power backs.
WKOutside zone and spread passing — heavy linemen struggle to reach quick edge defenders. Their pass protection can be exploited by athletic speed rushers on the outside.

Team Archetypes & Adaptation

Great WRs, Average QB

Run the ball to set up play-action. Use short, accurate routes (slant, flat, crossing) that your QB can throw accurately. Let your WRs win yards after the catch rather than asking your QB to throw into tight windows. Avoid four-verticals and deep comeback concepts that expose arm limitations.

Elite QB, Poor WRs

Run the ball first. Use the TE as the primary receiving option — he faces linebacker coverage rather than cornerback coverage. Target your best WR exclusively in situations where he has clear leverage: red zone fades, hitch against soft coverage. Protect your QB with maximum blocking and avoid situations that require his WR to win contested deep balls.

Great RB, Weak Passing Game

Run the ball 60%+ of the time. Use play-action aggressively — even a poor WR group becomes dangerous when linebackers commit to the run fake. Protect leads with clock control. Your passing game should be built entirely around quick, short, high-percentage throws that ask the minimum of a limited WR group.
